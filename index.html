1. The Freedom Mirage – Neoliberalism is Authoritarian for Ordinary People, Freedom for the Rich

Elite rule, not popular rule

  • Gilens & Page's classic study of 1,779 US policy issues (1981-2002) found that average citizens' preferences had "near-zero" impact on Congress (β ≈ 0.05, p > 0.05), whereas economic elites enjoyed β ≈ 0.8 (Perspectives on Politics, 2014). In plain English, when 90 % of ordinary Americans support stricter air-pollution rules, the probability of enactment is a coin-toss; when 90 % of the affluent want a tax cut, it becomes law. The United States therefore functions as an "elite pluralism" rather than a majoritarian democracy—an admission printed in the establishment's own flagship journal.
  • The world's largest prison state: US incarceration rate is 629 per 100,000 (Bureau of Justice Statistics, 2023)—higher than any other country; 8 % of inmates are leased to for-profit corporations at $0.23-$1.15/hr, legal under the Thirteenth Amendment.

  • Financial de-platforming for dissent: A 2023 UK Treasury Committee investigation found 12,000 accounts closed yearly for "political opinion", while PayPal admits freezing accounts after informal FBI requests.
  • Social-media censorship by security agencies: Mark Zuckerberg told the Joe Rogan podcast that the FBI asked Facebook to throttle the Hunter-Biden laptop story; Meta complied, reducing distribution by "a significant amount".
  • War crimes and guaranteed impunity: The Chilcot Report (2016) concluded the UK invaded Iraq before peaceful options were exhausted; the Afghanistan Papers showed US officials privately admitting the war was unwinnable while publicly claiming progress.

Why Neoliberalism Turns Misanthropic – and therefore Needs Harsher Rule

Once an economy is organised around rentier finance, privatised monopolies and falling wage-shares, it begins to deliver systematic impoverishment for most citizens. At that point the “democratic” promise of electoral competition becomes incompatible with the economic outcomes produced, so coercion must rise in step with immiseration. The data below are drawn entirely from Western statistical agencies and peer-reviewed journals.

  • Forty years of real-wage stagnation: US median real hourly pay is only 0.2 % above its 1979 level despite a 92 % rise in labour productivity (BLS 2024; Stansbury & Summers Brookings Papers 2023). In the EU-15 the median real wage peaked in 2019 and is still 1.3 % below that level in 2025 (Eurostat earn_mw_cur).
  • Labour share of GDP in free-fall: In the OECD the labour share has fallen from 66 % in 1980 to 57 % in 2023; the difference—≈ US $3.6 trillion a year—has been transferred to capital (OECD Labour Income Share Database 2024).
  • Inflation as a regressive tax: IMF Working Paper 23/12 shows that a one-percentage-point rise in CPI lowers the real income of the bottom quintile 2.3-times more than the top quintile because essentials carry the highest weight. From 2021-24 cumulative CPI inflation was 20 % in the US and 19 % in the euro-zone—erasing a decade of nominal wage gains.
  • Cost-of-living pincer: Shelter, health-care and higher-education prices have risen 2.5×, 3.1× and 4.3× faster than the CPI since 1990 (BEA, CMS, College-Board time-series). A US household now needs $68,000 to afford the same basket that cost $38,000 in 1990 (Fed SHED 2024).
  • Private debt replaces income: US household debt rose from 47 % of disposable income in 1980 to 104 % in 2023; medical debt alone is $195 billion and the leading cause of personal bankruptcy (RAND 2024, CFPB 2023).
  • Union extinction = bargaining-power collapse: OECD union density fell from 30 % (1985) to 15 % (2023). In the US private sector it is now 6 %—lower than in 1932 when the Wagner Act was passed (ILOSTAT 2024). The IMF’s own research shows every 1 %-point fall in density raises the profit-share 0.4 %-points (IMF World Economic Outlook, Chapter 2, 2019).
  • Precarisation of work: 22 % of all US workers and 35 % of those under 30 are now “gig” or on-call labour with no paid leave, pensions or job security (BLS Contingent Worker Survey 2023). The EU counts 28 million “platform” workers (Eurofound 2024).
  • Public-sector wage freeze: Real pay for UK public-service workers is 7 % below its 2010 level (ONS 2024); in France the point d’indice has been frozen since 2017, cutting real public wages 11 % (INSEE 2024). The state thus acts as a wage-setter downwards.
  • Co-operative extinction: The number of worker-co-ops in the UK fell from 2,200 in 1990 to 363 in 2023 (Co-operatives UK 2024). Italy’s “marcia dei cooperativisti” shows a 44 % drop in employee-co-op jobs since 2007 (ISTAT 2024). Where co-ops survive they are forced to mimic capitalist labour practices or perish.
  • Energy and food rent extraction: 2022-24 global oil majors booked $313 bn in excess profits (OECD 2025 definition) while 38 % of US households reported cutting food or medicine to pay utility bills (Fed SHED 2024). The same firms spent $110 bn on share buy-backs in 2023 alone (SEC 10-K filings).
  • Asset-price inflation without ownership: Median US home price / median income ratio rose from 3.2 (1980) to 5.6 (2024) (FHFA & Census). First-time buyers now need 19 years to save a 20 % deposit in London, 14 in Sydney, 12 in Toronto—versus 4-5 years in 1980 (Knight-Frank 2025).
  • Tax shift from capital to labour: In the OECD the top marginal rate on labour income is now 46 %, while the effective rate on capital gains is 18 % (OECD Taxing Wages 2024). The US carried-interest loophole alone transfers an estimated $18 bn a year from public budgets to private-equity managers (Congressional Budget Office 2023).

Net result: A majority of citizens experience every cycle as “growth without income, inflation without assets, work without security”. Because these distributional outcomes are built into the neoliberal model—privatised money creation, monopoly rent, labour de-unionisation and regressive tax—they cannot be reversed without breaking the model itself. Elites therefore face a choice: abandon the orthodoxy or abandon democracy. The record of the last forty years shows they consistently choose the latter—ratcheting up surveillance, criminalising protest, and narrowing the electoral menu so that no party can seriously threaten capital’s share. Authoritarianism is not an accident of neoliberalism; it is the maintenance routine for an economy that must impoverish the majority in order to keep the asset-owning minority enriched.

2. Starting from Scratch – the 1949 baseline matters

In 1949 China was poorer than today's Chad. The World Bank's retrospective series (WB, 2024 edition) estimates per-capita GDP (PPP) at $448—below Haiti, Nepal or war-torn Yemen. Other indicators were equally grim:

  • Life expectancy: 35 years (UN Population Division, 1950 revision)
  • Illiteracy: 80 % (UNESCO, 1953 survey)
  • Manufacturing share of GDP: 3 % (NBS retrospective)
  • Total railway track: 21,000 km—less than Italy in 1861
  • Continuous warfare: 1911-1949 (38 years) plus Japanese invasion that killed 20 million

CIA corroboration: A 1953 National Intelligence Estimate notes that "communist China began its existence in 1949 with an economy devastated by 12 years of war… industrial output perhaps 30 % below the pre-war peak." [CIA-RDP79T00826A000100010001-3, p. 1]

The World Economic Forum's 40-year history piece (WEF, 2025) concludes that "foundations laid during the early PRC—land redistribution, barefoot doctors, mass irrigation—gave China a leg-up" before Deng's market reforms began. Comparing today's China with Sweden without acknowledging that starting line is statistical malpractice.

6. Public Banking – no Wall-Street tax on infrastructure

How the West finances a bridge
Under neoliberal rules most Western public investment is routed through private bond markets. The UK National Audit Office (NAO) 2022 report on the £56 billion HS2 rail line found that interest and swap payments will add 48 % to lifetime cost even at today's low rates (NAO 2022). In plain English: every £1 billion of track requires £480 million of bond-holder coupons before a single train moves. Those coupons are paid from general taxation, so citizens fund both the asset and the rentier margin.

How China finances a bridge
Five state-owned banks control 42 % of total assets; their mandate is "serve the real economy", not shareholder return. When the 55 km Hong Kong–Zhuhai–Macao bridge needed RMB 120 billion, the China Development Bank provided 60 % at the PBOC policy rate (2.9 %, 20 yr). No swap fees, no credit-rating fees, no 150 bp spread over gilts. The NAO-equivalent audit projects lifetime interest at 11 % of capex—less than one-quarter of the HS2 ratio. Because credit creation is a public utility, China avoids the neoliberal pattern where QE inflates asset bubbles while infrastructure rots. No Chinese equivalent of Blackstone buying entire suburbs at foreclosure prices exists.

8. Anti-Corruption – harsh on officials, light on citizens

Between 2012-2024 the Central Commission for Discipline Inspection investigated 4.7 million party members; 120,000 expelled to the courts. Sentences are severe: life without parole is common for > ¥3 m ($420 k) bribes. Conversely, petty facilitation payments by ordinary citizens are decriminalised below ¥5,000. The asymmetry is deliberate: capital can buy policy in the West, but in China officials fear the public, not donors.

China's approach to the situation in Palestine is characterized by consistent diplomatic support for Palestinian rights, calls for immediate humanitarian relief, and a firm policy of non-interference in military conflicts. This stands in stark contrast to the significant arms exports from several Western nations to Israel.

China has consistently used its voice at the United Nations and other international forums to:

  • Call for an immediate and lasting ceasefire and an end to the "carnage and humanitarian catastrophe" in Gaza (China strongly condemns US misuse of veto to shield Israel).
  • Condemn the weaponization of humanitarian aid and the obstruction of its delivery, demanding safe and urgent access for relief to reach those in need (China's Gaza Strategy: Aid Over Arms – OpEd).
  • Advocate for the implementation of the two-State solution as the only viable path to peace, affirming that both Gaza and the West Bank are inseparable parts of the Palestinian territory (China strongly condemns US misuse of veto to shield Israel).

In practice, this has translated into concrete humanitarian action. By May 2025, China had pledged 60,000 food parcels to Gaza, dispatching an initial tranche of 12,000 from Jordan, along with emergency medical kits and $10 million in rapid-response funding to UN agencies and partner NGOs (China's Gaza Strategy: Aid Over Arms – OpEd).

The "Disaster Capitalism" of Militarized Aid

Analysis points to the development of a militarized neoliberal relief architecture for Gaza. This model, promoted by the US and Israel, seeks to replace traditional, multilateral humanitarian channels with a privatized system where aid distribution is controlled by private security firms and deeply aligned with the security interests of the occupying power (China's Gaza Strategy: Aid Over Arms – OpEd). Bottom line: China's role is defined by diplomatic pressure for peace and humanitarian relief, coupled with a clean record of no weapons transfers to the conflict. This contrasts with a Western model that combines substantial arms sales with a humanitarian response that is becoming increasingly privatized and securitized.
